Upcoming Cross-Country Bike Ride Promotes Cancer Awareness

October 30, 2020 - Matthew Novelli

Willard Sullivan takes every opportunity he gets to ride his bike. Even in the dead of Illinois winter, you can find him biking to class. This summer, Sullivan plans to take his rides to a new level by biking from New York to California—and he’s not alone.

Sullivan is a member of “Illini 4000,” a registered student organization at the University of Illinois that holds annual cross-country bike rides. The rides – held since 2007 – aim to raise money for cancer-focused charities and promote cancer awareness. Each rider has a goal of raising at least $4,000 before the ride–so far this year’s 21 riders have collectively raised over $55,000 according to their website. The funds are then gathered and donated to various cancer-oriented charities and research groups including “Camp Kesem,” which provides “free week-long summer camp experience and year-round peer support” to families affected by cancer. In 2018, the team donated over $118,000 to charities according to their annual financial report.

Sullivan’s affinity for mountain biking and road biking runs in the family. “I’ve always been interested in biking across America because my dad did it when he was younger. That’s actually how I first found the club.” The charity and social aspects of the club are bonuses for Sullivan, as he says, “honestly I’m most excited about meeting new people and creating a stronger bond with my team.”

The three-month ride consists of 70 stops strewn across New York and San Francisco. The stops – called “stayovers” – are community centers, churches, YMCAs, and anyone willing to house the riders for the night. You can even send the riders mail along the route. Along the way, the group carries out interviews with Americans affected with cancer and compiles them into the “Portraits Project,” a branch of Illini 4000. Stayover hosts are instructed to assemble local cancer survivors, patients and family members to tell stories for the project. Then, interviews–or “portraits”–are compiled on the Portraits Project website. However, Sullivan tells me a lot of these interviews happen by chance. “A lot of times it’s just random people off the street, like if the team stops at a gas station sometimes people walk up and ask what Illini 4000 is. Sometimes they’ve personally had cancer or someone they’ve known has had cancer.”

According to the American Cancer Society, cancer is usually found in older people with “80% of all cancers in the United Stated diagnosed in people 55 years of age or older.” Since cancer is classified as a group of diseases, “more than 1.8 million new cancer cases are expected to be diagnosed in 2020.”

The ride was originally planned for Summer 2020 but was postponed due to the COVID-19 pandemic. A shorter ride was scheduled later in the summer but was also cancelled. Sullivan says the team is still determined to complete the ride this summer by hosting meetings and training sessions online. “Everything is virtual… if you feel comfortable joining an in-person workout session we have those up to five people.” There’s even a plan to host virtual interviews with cancer survivors for the Portraits Project. The team’s virtual workout sessions consist of 2-hour trainings on the weekend accompanied by 1-hour midweek trainings. The regiment includes running and sit-ups – more than enough to prepare riders to bike across the country.

For 2021, the group is recycling the unused route from 2020. Each rider was tasked with finding at least 2 out of the 70 stayovers on the route, a difficult task since they’re essentially asking for accommodation free of charge. Sullivan tells me, “I was assigned two stayovers somewhere in Washington last year… what I did was I opened Google Maps and started scrolling around the town, seeing what kinds of establishments we could stay in.” Many places were receptive to the group, and some riders had personal connections to the stayovers. Others weren’t so kind, as Sullivan described calling a golf course that replied, “well what do you think we’re supposed to do?”

Doubt lingers in the air as to whether the ride will happen this summer. “We’re taking a huge risk here… I would be pretty frustrated if it doesn’t happen this year, but very understanding.” It’s clear from speaking with Sullivan that he’s passionate about his team and their mission.

“Illini 4000 is extremely special in the way it promotes cancer research but also promotes team bonding… I don’t know of any other organization that does a trip of this magnitude – and is successful.”

The team hits the road May 22nd, 2021, pandemic permitting.
