Students Grapple with COVID-19’s Life-Changing Effects

November 13, 2020 - Matthew Novelli

As colleges and universities around the United States near the end of the Fall semester, students are reflecting on the changes to their academic lives brought on by the COVID-19 pandemic. Many schools have flip-flopped between in-person and online instruction, and some students caught in the crossfire can’t help but feel left behind. Other students feel the transition to online learning has benefitted them, especially those who feel they’re technologically inclined. One thing is clear – the American college experience will never be the same.

In 2020, an unprecedented amount of schools switched to online instruction. However, the Pew Research Center’s 2020 American Trends Panel found that about 68% of U.S. adults would disagree when asked if “a course taken only online provides an equal educational value compared with a course taken in person in a classroom.” This is especially felt by remote students. Jason Zou is a student studying computer engineering at the University of Illinois. However, Zou isn’t living on campus for the Fall semester. Instead, he does all his schoolwork remotely from his home in the Philadelphia suburbs. Zou said he agreed with data, but it was less about the lecture itself and more about the experience. “I think on the surface, things like lectures are fairly similar in terms of effect… everything else is kind of made a little more difficult. Things like casually asking someone sitting next to you about something – you can’t really do that.”

Another poll conducted by Pew Research shows U.S. adults with better educations are more likely to say their lives have “changed in a major way” because of the pandemic. “I think it’s definitely affected my motivation… I’m a lot lazier now because I don’t really have to get out of bed, I can just skip class and watch [lectures] later,” says Vance Ursiny, an undergraduate student at the University of Illinois studying Chemical Engineering. He added on, suggesting there is a silver lining: “I also think that studying has been a lot easier in the way that most professors upload their lecture slides and videos of their lectures.” This response was somewhat surprising, as Pew Research notes that “College graduates are among the most likely to say that online classes do not measure up to in-person ones.”

The toll on students isn’t just academic – many students are finding online classes negatively affecting their motivation and emotional wellbeing. Ursiny says, “I definitely feel a lot less energy lately, but I can say for a fact a lot of my friends have been affected negatively and have been dealing with some deflated emotions as a result.” Zou, on the other hand, says his emotional wellbeing “has overall been kind of worse, I don’t really have any casual day-to-day interactions… your sense of time really changes when all you do is wake up and go 3 feet to your right.”

The COVID-19 pandemic is currently worsening in the United States, and as students prepare for Fall break, colleges will once again be faced with the decision between in-person and online instruction.
